The main difference between tokens and coins is that digital tokens live on blockchains that already exist while digital coins have a specific and unique blockchain that they run on. While various tokens can be built on top of the blockchain of a coin, a new coin cannot be built on top of a token’s protocol. Understanding the distinctions between tokens and coins are crucial in order to fully grasp digital assets and cryptocurrencies.

## What Are Coins?
Coins serve as the foundational blockchains on which different applications utilizing unique tokens are built upon. We can think of coins as the buildings of blockchain, while tokens are the offices that may come and go throughout a building’s history. In recent years, far more tokens have been created than blockchains due to the complexity involved in creating an entirely new blockchain. Besides taking more time to develop, creating a completely new blockchain entails searching for miners to verify and maintain the network, which would need to be meticulously fortified against [different attacks](https://medium.com/jibrel-network/6-network-hacks-you-need-to-know-aeb1077c23fb). Yet perhaps the biggest argument for creating your own coin on an entirely new blockchain versus going with an existing solution is the freedom from having to depend on outside technical teams for improvements that are out of your own control. 

### Use Cases of Coins

With the development of Ethereum and smart contract technology, alternative coins, known as altcoins, were created.  Each of these altcoins utilized its own blockchain and had its own specific use cases. 

**[Ethereum](https://www.ethereum.org):** A platform which is used to build other protocols on and what most ICOs in 2017 were built on. It uses Ether to maintain all network transactions, which is paid whenever any token built on top of it is sent or rewards are distributed to miners supporting the network.  

**[Ripple](https://ripple.com):** Used for real-time settlement, remittance and currency exchange utilizing the decentralized native cryptocurrency known as XRP.

**[Bitcoin:](https://bitcoin.org/en/)** Money transfer, payment, unit of account and store of value coin. 

**[NEO:](https://neo.org)** Also known as the “Ethereum” of China, NEO has a number of tokens built on top of it. NEO holders receive GAS as a form of dividends, which is also considered as payment when transactions are conducted on the network. 

## What Are Tokens?
Tokens, on the other hand, are blockchain agnostic, allowing the holder to perform a myriad of tasks within the realms of a particular blockchain. The most famous examples of tokens are [ERC-20](https://coinsutra.com/what-is-erc20-token/) tokens that were built on top of Ethereum. Creating tokens requires spending the coin of a particular blockchain to validate a token’s creation as well as any and all transactions. This is why ICOs (Initial Coin Offerings) raised funds in Ether “coin” and distributed their project’s “token” to their investors. Tokens can represent a variety of functions and may facilitate fractional asset ownership, settlement etc. 

## Use Case of Tokens
The birth of Ethereum and smart contract technology in 2015 provided developers the ability to launch new projects without having to build entirely new protocols and opened the way for crypto to be used for something other than payments. Currently there are three different types of tokens: 

### 1. Utility Tokens


*   Access Tokens: Applications that are used to make API requests on behalf of users. These tokens represent the authorization of a specific application to access particular parts of a user’s data.
*   Work Tokens: Such tokens rely on the service provider staking the native token of the network to earn the right to perform work. Increased usage of the network causes an increase in the price of the token. As demand for the service grows, more revenue will flow to service providers.
*   Governance Tokens: Governance tokens allow holders to vote on changes in the network to which they belong to. Typically, the number of tokens one holds is proportional to the number of votes they have.

### 2. Non-Fungible Tokens 
Utility tokens are primarily fungible, meaning that one ZRX token is the same as another. However, there are also non-fungible tokens (NFTs). This is an important distinction as it means that the same token can be endowed with different attributes. Therefore, NFTs may provide the most immediately obvious use cases for tokens. Allowing tokens to have different characteristics provides a myriad of possibilities. These include the purchase of items in video games, as well as use cases in transportation and ticketing. 

### 3. Security Tokens
Security tokens function similarly as securities and generally include projects whereby dividends or equity are granted to token holders. Security Token Offerings (STOs) can be broken down into subsets such as ETOs (Equity Token Offerings) and DTOs (Debt Token Offerings), enabling the tokenization of practically any element of the existing financial system. Yet perhaps the biggest benefit of security tokens is the ability for instant and permissionless trading. This differs from stock markets, which are only open during business hours and do not allow for trading of hard-to-split assets such as real estate. Since security tokens are easily divisible and operate in 24/7 markets, such problems cease to exist. For the first time, investors can own .01% of a Picasso or a building, which they can then easily sell to someone else.

## Coin Swaps: Transitioning from Token to Coin
In the ICO mania of 2017, many of the projects raising money that saw their price skyrocket were ERC-20 tokens built on top of the Ethereum blockchain. For teams that wanted to raise money fast, creating tokens instead of coins proved to be a wise choice. Since then, certain project like [Icon](https://icon.foundation) and [Vechain](https://www.vechain.org) have conducted what are known as [coin swaps,](https://en.bitcoinwiki.org/wiki/Coin_Swap) migrating onto their own blockchains and becoming coins. During a swap, holders and users can exchange their tokens for coins to be used on the new [mainnet.](https://coincentral.com/what-is-a-mainnet/) Such swaps are typically supported by large exchanges like Binance, making the process automatic for their users. Yet for those keeping their tokens safe in [their own wallets](jwallet.network), tokens must be manually exchanged. 

Ultimately, digital coins and tokens are essential parts of cryptocurrencies and digital assets. Understanding the differences between the two is essential for any investor in the cryptocurrency space. Not only can events like coin swaps and mainnet launches have massive impacts on a coin’s price, understanding the ecosystem as a whole can provide yet another avenue when evaluating the potential of a project.
